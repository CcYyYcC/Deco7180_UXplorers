# User Testing Report – Round 1

## 1. Overview

This round of user testing involved **11 participants**, including postgraduate students and young urban users who frequently rely on digital platforms to search for dining options. The testing aimed to evaluate how users interact with the prototype’s core features, how they make dining decisions in realistic contexts, and what factors influence their trust in the system.

Participants demonstrated diverse decision-making styles, ranging from **data-driven (e.g., Oliver)** to **efficiency-focused (e.g., Yuming Zhong)** and **automation-oriented (e.g., Brian Guo)**. Despite these differences, consistent behavioral patterns emerged, indicating shared priorities in efficiency, clarity, and practicality.

---

## 2. Core Feature Evaluation

The **route planning and DIY itinerary feature** was the strongest aspect of the prototype. **9 out of 11 participants (≈82%)** expressed clear willingness to use this feature, particularly in travel or exploration scenarios. Users noted that integrating restaurant discovery into a planned route reduces both cognitive effort and decision time, making it highly valuable in unfamiliar environments. Additionally, **7 users (≈64%)** preferred modifying existing routes rather than creating new ones, suggesting a strong preference for a **“generate → adjust” workflow**.

The **food safety rating feature** was also widely recognised as important. **10 participants (≈91%)** considered it a key factor in decision-making, and **7 users (≈64%)** indicated that low safety scores would directly eliminate a restaurant from consideration. However, several users noted that in Australia, safety functions more as a **threshold filter** rather than a primary ranking factor. A smaller portion (**≈18%**) raised concerns about the ethical implications of visually highlighting low scores (e.g., using red indicators).

In contrast, the **weight adjustment feature** showed limited effectiveness. **8 users (≈73%)** found it unintuitive or unnecessary, often describing it as too abstract or unclear. Instead, users preferred familiar mechanisms such as **sorting and filtering**, indicating a preference for clear and direct interaction over complex parameter tuning.

---

## 3. Decision-Making Factors

Decision-making was strongly influenced by practical constraints. The most dominant factor was **waiting time**, identified by **10 users (≈91%)** as a critical concern and often described as a “dealbreaker”. Even highly rated restaurants were rejected if waiting times were too long.

**Walking distance** was also significant, with **8 users (≈73%)** emphasising the need to minimise travel effort, especially in time-constrained situations.

At the same time, **taste and rating remained universal drivers**, with **all 11 participants (100%)** indicating that food quality ultimately determines their final choice. These findings suggest that users balance quality, safety, and effort, but will reject options that exceed acceptable thresholds.

---

## 4. Missing Features

A critical gap identified across all participants (**100%**) is the absence of **category filtering**. Users typically begin their decision process by deciding what they want to eat, and without cuisine-based filtering, the route planning feature loses practical value.

Additionally, **8 users (≈73%)** highlighted missing **practical dining information**, including menus, price range, opening hours, and real-time queue conditions. These details are essential for final decision-making.

Another key need is **context-aware recommendation**, with **7 users (≈64%)** expressing interest in scenario-based modes. For example:
- Solo mode: prioritising fast service and individual seating  
- Group mode: prioritising space and social environments  

---

## 5. Trust & Social Validation

Trust was identified as a critical factor. **All participants (100%)** emphasised the importance of **data transparency**, particularly for food safety ratings. Users expect clear attribution to official sources and are sceptical of systems without verifiable data.

Furthermore, **7 users (≈64%)** expressed concerns about **commercial bias or hidden advertising**, suggesting that trust can be easily undermined.

When interacting with shared routes or user-generated content, **8 users (≈73%)** prioritised **authenticity over presentation quality**. Users preferred:
- Real, unedited photos  
- Time-specific information (e.g., queue duration)  
- Honest, experience-based reviews  

---

## 6. Behavioral Patterns

Several behavioural patterns emerged.

First, users follow a **goal-oriented decision flow**, starting with category selection, then evaluating rating and distance, and finally considering constraints such as waiting time.

Second, **≈73% of users** preferred a **hybrid interaction model**, combining system-generated suggestions with manual adjustments.

Finally, users showed strong resistance to **information overload**, preferring simple and direct interactions over complex controls.

---

## 7. Limitations

The study has several limitations. The sample size is relatively small (**n=11**), and participants are primarily students, which may limit generalisability. Additionally, testing was conducted using a prototype rather than a fully functional system.

---

## 8. Conclusion

The findings indicate that users strongly value **efficiency, clarity, and practicality** in dining decision tools. The route planning feature is the key strength of the prototype, while category filtering and practical information are critical missing elements.

Users prefer simple and familiar interaction patterns over complex customisation, and trust depends heavily on **data transparency and authenticity**.

Future iterations should focus on:
- Strengthening goal-oriented filtering  
- Enhancing route-based decision support  
- Providing practical information  
- Building transparent and trustworthy data systems
