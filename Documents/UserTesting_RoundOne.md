User Testing Report – Round 1
1. Overview
This round of user testing involved 11 participants, including postgraduate students and young urban users who regularly use digital platforms to search for dining options. The testing focused on evaluating how users interact with the prototype’s core features, how they make dining decisions in real-world contexts, and what factors influence their trust in the system.
Overall, participants demonstrated diverse preferences in terms of decision-making styles, ranging from data-driven (e.g., Oliver) to efficiency-focused (e.g., Yuming Zhong) and automation-oriented (e.g., Brian Guo). Despite these differences, several consistent behavioral patterns emerged across the sample.
 
2. Core Feature Evaluation
The route planning and DIY itinerary feature was the most positively received aspect of the prototype. Out of 11 participants, 9 users (≈82%) stated that they would use this feature, particularly in travel or exploration scenarios. Many users highlighted that integrating restaurant discovery into an existing route significantly reduces cognitive effort and planning time. Additionally, 7 users (≈64%) expressed a preference for modifying existing routes rather than creating one from scratch, suggesting that users favor a “generate → adjust” workflow.
The food safety rating feature was also widely acknowledged as valuable. 10 out of 11 users (≈91%) considered food safety an important factor in decision-making, and 7 users (≈64%) indicated that a low safety score would immediately disqualify a restaurant regardless of its taste rating. However, several participants noted that in contexts like Australia, where baseline hygiene standards are relatively high, safety acts more as a threshold filter rather than a primary ranking factor. A small number of users (≈18%) also raised concerns about the ethical implications of visually emphasizing low ratings (e.g., using red indicators).
In contrast, the weight adjustment feature received relatively weak feedback. 8 users (≈73%) found the feature unintuitive or unnecessary, often describing it as “too abstract” or “unclear in effect.” Instead, participants consistently expressed a preference for more familiar interaction methods such as sorting (e.g., by rating) or filtering (e.g., by popularity or category). This suggests that users are more comfortable with concrete, goal-oriented controls than with flexible but complex parameter tuning.
 
3. Decision-Making Factors
Across all participants, decision-making was strongly influenced by a small set of practical constraints. The most prominent factor was waiting time, mentioned by 10 users (≈91%) as a critical concern. Many participants described long queues as a “dealbreaker,” indicating that even highly rated restaurants would be avoided if waiting times were excessive.
Walking distance was another key factor, with 8 users (≈73%) emphasizing the importance of minimizing travel effort, especially in tight schedules or unfamiliar environments. This was particularly evident in users like Yuming, who prioritize efficiency during short breaks.
At the same time, taste and rating remained fundamental drivers, with all 11 users (100%) indicating that food quality ultimately determines their final choice. While safety is important, participants repeatedly emphasized that “good food” is still the primary motivation for dining decisions.
These findings suggest that users balance quality, safety, and effort, but will reject options that exceed acceptable thresholds in time or distance.
 
4. Missing Features
One of the most critical gaps identified in the prototype is the lack of category filtering. All 11 participants (100%) indicated that this is an essential feature. Users typically begin their decision process by asking “what do I want to eat,” and without cuisine-based filtering (e.g., Chinese, seafood, café), the route planning feature loses much of its practical value.
In addition, 8 users (≈73%) highlighted the absence of practical dining information, including menus, price range, opening hours, and real-time queue conditions. These details are considered essential for final decision-making, as they directly affect feasibility and expectations.
Another emerging need is context-aware recommendation, with 7 users (≈64%) expressing interest in features that adapt to different social scenarios. For example, users suggested a “solo mode” that prioritizes quick service and individual seating, and a “group mode” that favors spacious environments suitable for social interaction.
 
5. Trust & Social Validation
Trust in the system was identified as a crucial factor influencing user adoption. All 11 participants (100%) emphasized the importance of data transparency, particularly for food safety ratings. Users expect clear attribution to official sources (e.g., government inspection data) and expressed skepticism toward systems that lack verifiable references.
Furthermore, 7 users (≈64%) raised concerns about potential commercial bias or hidden advertising, indicating that trust can be easily undermined if recommendations appear sponsored or manipulated.
When interacting with shared routes or user-generated content, 8 users (≈73%) stated that they value authenticity over curation. Specifically, users prefer:
•	Real, unedited food photos 
•	Time-specific information (e.g., actual queue duration) 
•	Honest, experience-based reviews 
This highlights that social validation is not just about quantity (ratings), but about perceived authenticity.
 
6. Behavioral Patterns
Several consistent behavioral patterns emerged from the testing.
First, users follow a goal-oriented decision process, typically starting with category selection, then evaluating rating and distance, and finally considering constraints such as waiting time. This structured approach suggests that users prefer clear, sequential decision support rather than flexible but complex systems.
Second, a majority of users (≈73%) demonstrated a preference for a hybrid interaction model, where system-generated recommendations are combined with manual adjustments (e.g., drag-and-drop route editing). This reflects a desire for both efficiency and control.
Finally, users showed strong resistance to information overload. Features that required interpretation or adjustment (such as weight sliders) were often ignored in favor of simpler, more direct interactions. This indicates that usability should prioritize clarity and immediacy over flexibility.
 
7. Limitations
This study has several limitations. The sample size is relatively small (n=11), and participants are primarily students, which may limit the generalizability of the findings. Additionally, testing was conducted using a prototype rather than a fully functional system, meaning that some feedback is based on perceived rather than actual usage.
 
8. Conclusion
Overall, the findings indicate that users strongly value efficiency, clarity, and practicality in dining decision tools. The route planning feature represents a key strength of the prototype, particularly when combined with nearby recommendations. However, the system currently lacks essential features such as category filtering and practical dining information, which are critical for real-world usability.
Importantly, users prefer simple, familiar interaction patterns over complex customization, and their trust in the system depends heavily on data transparency and authenticity.
These insights suggest that the next iteration should focus on:
•	Strengthening goal-oriented filtering 
•	Enhancing route-based decision support 
•	Providing practical, real-world information 
•	Building transparent and trustworthy data systems
